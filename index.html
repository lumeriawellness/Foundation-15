A word before you start

As women in midlife, we are often exhausted, stressed, and struggling in a very real way with the changes our bodies are going through. We would like clarity on exactly the one or few things causing the problem and would love a magic pill to fix it all. The reality is both more complex and more beautiful at the same time.

We are organic beings designed to be in tune with nature — whole foods, sunlight, movement, sleeping and living in circadian rhythm. We are also gifted with a highly functioning cognitive capacity that shapes how we experience our entire existence. Our worldview, thoughts, and perspectives can make or break a moment and, for better or worse, a lifetime.

Health is not an accomplishment. It is a reflection of how we live. How we live is a reflection of our daily decisions to align our priorities with our actions. It is a continuum of thought and action that brings us back to homeostasis.

At first glance this list might look like a full-time job. It is not. Many of these are things you are already doing in some form. What we are doing here is naming them, refining them, and making them intentional. A sunrise walk you already take becomes your circadian anchor. The deep breath you grab between tasks becomes your mindfulness practice. The vegetables on your dinner plate become your estrobolome support. These are not additions to your life. They are upgrades to what is already there.

Sometimes the things we do that keep us from better health — scrolling instead of going to bed early, having a glass of wine instead of a cup of tea, doing something productive instead of exercising — are a reflection of our desire to fulfill needs that become pressing when they haven’t been met. Scrolling requires nearly zero effort and gives us a dopamine hit, a way to check out, a false sense of connection. Wine is an instant sense of stress release, literally popping the cork after a long day of nonstop going and doing and being everything for everyone. The issue is that none of these have lasting benefits. We wake up each day feeling no better, in fact a little worse than the day before. Microdoses of deficit add up over the years.

We are not broken when we feel exhausted, stressed, heavy, pained, and struggle to experience joy. Our job is not to criticize, condemn, or dissect ourselves. It is to nourish ourselves with the actual substance required to be in homeostasis. One day at a time, one habit at a time, practiced consistently over time — the impact in the years to come is profound. Start low and go slow. Titrate to effect.

Pregame Prep

Mindset shifts — an invitation to presence, purpose, and power

The art of becoming well requires the consistent commitment to microdoses of health promoting habits, and the omission of disease promoting ones. The ability to “make it stick” and to feel a sense of peace in all the growing and doing and being relies on frequently accessing and returning to a wisdom mindset. The goal is not another plan to follow. It is change that becomes second nature, woven into who you are rather than added onto what you do. That requires understanding the why behind your choices — the ones that serve you, and especially the ones that don’t. That introspection is different for everyone and it is everything.

Presence: “Settle in”

This present moment — with these littles, this family, these friends, in this home, under this sky, on this day of this year — IS your life. If, like me, you’ve ever suffered from “I’ll be happy when…” syndrome then you know that being present can be very difficult for a woman carrying what feels like the weight of the world. And at the same time, full immersion in the present is so powerful. It’s not always possible. Women can multitask like the best of them for a reason, because that weight is heavy, and balls drop fast. But when you can, breathe. Catch yourself in the chaos and bring yourself back to now. Savor it.

Purpose: “See the truth”

The reality is that not every moment feels good. In fact, a lot of moments throughout each and every day are hard. The tantrums, the request you’ve repeated a thousand times (why can’t clothes just magically float to the hamper?), holding a sick baby (no matter how old they are), helping your kid through a hard moment, going to work when you’d rather be home, waking up when you’re not fully rested, getting in a workout when your joints aren’t feeling it, and all of it. All of the hard. During times like this I find myself looking up. How is this moment part of my formation as a human? What is the lesson, the opportunity for wisdom, the virtue that needs strengthening? Romans 8:28 tells us that God uses all things for good. “Good” being our spiritual expansion, not necessarily our worldly pleasure. What is the spiritual truth in this moment?

Power: “Own the choice”

How we frame our habits, decisions, and lifestyle matters. The reality is that all of it is a choice. We have free will. Instead of “I can’t drink wine” try “I don’t want alcohol right now.” Instead of “I have to workout” try “I’m choosing my hard now.” Exercise when you don’t feel like it is hard, but being weak and in pain and lacking functional and cognitive ability later is arguably going to be much harder. Remember: microdoses matter.

01
Daily sunlight and nature
Circadian rhythm · Cortisol regulation · Vitamin D · Serotonin · Melatonin

Morning light entering your eyes within the first hour of waking is the most powerful signal your brain receives all day. It tells your master internal clock what time it is, triggering a precise cortisol peak that sets your energy, mood, alertness, and sleep timing for the next 16 hours. Go outside without sunglasses. Your eyes need direct, unfiltered light to signal the brain.

This is the first of three daily sunlight anchors. Midday light peaks serotonin synthesis and vitamin D production. Evening light, a walk at dusk or sitting near a window at sunset, tells your brain the day is ending and prepares melatonin to rise on schedule.

Most women in perimenopause are vitamin D deficient, which directly affects mood, immunity, bone density, and pain perception. Have your level tested if you haven’t recently.

There are micromoments throughout our day that we very easily bypass without even realizing it. Consider this an invitation to let nature be your reminder to breathe, to acknowledge something you’re grateful for, to remember that you are not carrying the entire world alone.
02
Hydration
Cellular hydration · Cortisol regulation · Gut motility · Electrolyte balance · Pain perception

After seven to eight hours without fluids, your body wakes mildly dehydrated. Even mild dehydration measurably impairs cognition, amplifies fatigue, and mimics hunger. Drinking water before your first cup of coffee rehydrates your cells, blunts the morning cortisol spike, and gives your brain what it needs to actually wake up.

Dehydration mimics hunger. Before reaching for a snack, drink 8 oz and wait 10 minutes. Dehydration also amplifies pain. Spinal discs, cartilage, and fascia are largely water. Chronic under-hydration increases joint friction and pain sensitivity.

On days you exercise or in summer heat, add a pinch of Himalayan salt or a clean electrolyte powder with no added sugar. Your daily target: half your body weight in ounces.

03
Fuel for function
Insulin sensitivity · Inflammation · Estrobolome · Gut microbiome · Blood sugar · Mediterranean pattern

Estrogen supports insulin receptor sensitivity, glucose uptake in skeletal muscle, and suppression of glucose production by the liver. As estrogen declines in perimenopause, insulin resistance worsens. The same dietary choices that were neutral at 35 carry real metabolic consequences at 45.

The Mediterranean dietary pattern has the strongest evidence base for reducing blood pressure, triglycerides, LDL cholesterol, and inflammatory markers in this population.

Gut health

The estrobolome, the specific gut bacteria responsible for estrogen metabolism, is fed by dietary fiber and disrupted by processed food and artificial additives. Aim for 25 to 35g of fiber daily from diverse plant sources, plus one to two servings of fermented food. Kimchi, sauerkraut, plain kefir, or full-fat Greek yogurt all count.

Move after meals

A 10-minute walk after each meal acts as an independent glucose transporter, pulling blood sugar into muscle cells without requiring insulin. If a walk is not possible, 20 air squats produce a comparable result. Any movement counts. A living room dance party clears glucose and cortisol at the same time. Science approves.

Added sugar

Added sugar is sugar that is not naturally present in a food. It is sugar added during processing, cooking, or at the table, found in obvious places like desserts and candy, and in less obvious places like flavored yogurt, salad dressing, pasta sauce, and coffee creamer. This is distinct from the sugar naturally occurring in fruit, vegetables, and dairy, which comes packaged with fiber, vitamins, minerals, and water that slow its absorption and support the body rather than burden it. Naturally occurring sugar in whole foods is not something to limit and remains an important part of a healthy diet.

The American Heart Association recommends women limit added sugar to no more than 25 grams, or about 6 teaspoons, per day. Beverages are one of the largest and easiest to overlook sources. A flavored coffee drink or a few tablespoons of flavored creamer can use up most or all of a day's allowance before any food is eaten.

To identify added sugar, read the Nutrition Facts label. Added sugar is listed separately from total sugar, directly underneath it, under the carbohydrate section. If a product does not have a Nutrition Facts label, check the ingredient list. Added sugar goes by many names: cane sugar, corn syrup, high-fructose corn syrup, honey, agave, molasses, dextrose, and anything ending in "ose."

04
Hit your protein target
Muscle preservation · Hormonal function · Bone density · Immune response · Metabolic health · Anabolic sensitivity

Protein is not just a fitness variable. It is a foundational requirement for how your body functions at the cellular level. Hormones, enzymes, immune proteins, neurotransmitters, skin, bone, and muscle are all protein-dependent structures. In midlife, your need for dietary protein increases precisely when most women are not paying attention to it. Declining estrogen reduces anabolic sensitivity, meaning your muscle tissue becomes less responsive to the protein you do eat. The result is accelerated muscle loss, slower metabolism, reduced bone density, thinning skin and hair, blunted immune response, and mood and cognitive changes that are easy to misattribute to hormones alone. Many of the symptoms women accept as inevitable in perimenopause have a significant nutritional component that goes unaddressed.

The target most current research supports is between 1.2 and 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight daily, significantly higher than the general recommendation. More important than the daily total is distribution. Your body can only use so much protein at one time for muscle repair and synthesis, so spreading intake across three meals, each with a meaningful protein source, matters as much as hitting your number. Prioritize complete proteins, foods that contain all essential amino acids your body cannot make on its own. Eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, salmon and fatty fish, lean poultry, and beef are your highest quality options. Fatty fish earns a top spot not just for its amino acid profile but for the anti-inflammatory omega-3s that work alongside protein to protect muscle tissue. If you limit animal products, whole soy foods like edamame, tempeh, and tofu are the only plant proteins with a comparable amino acid profile.

Think of protein less as a macronutrient to count and more as raw material your body is constantly drawing from. When supply is chronically low, the body pulls from its own tissue, muscle, bone, immune cells, to meet demand. In midlife, that is a deficit you cannot afford to run.

Pause on purpose
Parasympathetic activation · HRV · Cortisol · Attention restoration · Grounding

Start here. This is your most accessible tool, free to use anytime, anywhere, as many times as the day asks for it. No goal, no focus, no phone. Just stop and breathe.

Close your eyes and picture your bare feet in the grass. If possible, step outside and actually do this. Feel the wind on your face, the sun warm your skin. Research on grounding and nature exposure supports measurable reductions in cortisol, improved heart rate variability, and parasympathetic activation. This is medicine. Access it freely.

Body awareness practices, yoga, breathwork, tai chi, and somatic movement extend this practice for when you have more time. Even 10 minutes. Even imperfectly.

Stop. Breathe. Soak it in. Rinse and repeat as often as needed.
06
Zone 2 cardio — 30 minutes daily
Cardiovascular function · Insulin sensitivity · Mood · Sleep · Zone 2 physiology

Zone 2 cardio means moving at a conversational pace, brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or dancing, at an intensity where you could speak in full sentences but would not want to sing. This zone specifically targets mitochondrial efficiency, fat oxidation, and cardiovascular resilience.

The weekly target of approximately 150 minutes is well-supported in lifestyle medicine research for reducing cardiovascular risk, improving insulin sensitivity, stabilizing mood, and improving sleep quality in perimenopausal women.

The after-meal walk is one of the most natural ways to accumulate this time without adding a separate workout to your day. Ten minutes after each meal adds up to 30 minutes before you have done anything extra.

Brisk walking counts. Dancing counts. A bike ride counts. The bar for Zone 2 is lower than most women think, and the return is higher than almost any other single habit on this list.
07
Resistance training
Muscle preservation · Bone density · Metabolism · Insulin sensitivity · Mental health

You are already moving constantly. This is not about moving more. This is about moving for yourself, on purpose.

After 40, women lose muscle mass at an accelerating rate without resistance training. Muscle is your metabolic engine. It burns more energy at rest, absorbs blood sugar like a sponge, protects your joints, and preserves bone density as estrogen declines. Without it, metabolism slows, weight becomes harder to manage, pain increases, and fatigue deepens.

Research supports 2 to 3 sessions per week of resistance training, compound movements at moderate to high intensity, 6 to 8 reps per muscle group, with adequate rest between sessions. Ten to 15 focused minutes with bands, a TRX strap, or dumbbells meets the evidence threshold. You do not need a gym.

There is also a mental shift that happens when you move for yourself rather than for everyone else. It releases BDNF, reduces cortisol, and gives you something in the day that belongs only to you.

Setting up a small home gym, even just a corner of the living room with your equipment ready to go, significantly increases your chance of getting a workout in. If you are new to resistance training or want a structured starting point, there are excellent resources online and at home. Doing something is always better than nothing. Microdoses matter.
08
Your wind-down
Melatonin synthesis · Sleep architecture · Cortisol · HPA axis · Circadian rhythm · Progesterone

Sleep is not passive. It is the primary overnight repair window for every system in your body: immune, metabolic, cardiovascular, neurological, and hormonal. The brain cycles through distinct stages, each with its own purpose. Deep slow-wave sleep (NREM) drives physical repair, immune function, and memory consolidation. REM processes emotional experience and supports mood regulation. A full night allows four to six complete 90-minute cycles, which is why 7 to 9 hours is the target. Both stages are disrupted by alcohol, certain medications, sedatives, and the progesterone loss that characterizes perimenopause.

Timing matters as much as duration. Going to bed too late steals NREM. You miss your deepest, most physically restorative sleep and wake feeling heavy regardless of total hours. Waking too early steals REM. You feel foggy, reactive, and mentally flat. For women in perimenopause, hormonal shifts already compromise both, meaning many are getting enough hours but not enough restoration.

How well and when we sleep is not always in our control. Parents, caregivers, illness, work schedules, stress, and hormonal changes all affect timing and quality. What follows are the foundational steps to foster better sleep, with resources for the harder circumstances.

The Basics

Morning: Get outside or use a 10,000 lux light box within 30 minutes of waking. Same wake time daily, no exceptions. This anchors cortisol and melatonin timing for the entire day.

Evening: Switch to dim, warm, or red-spectrum light after 8 PM. Blue and white light suppress melatonin synthesis by 1.5 to 3 hours. A red or amber bulb, a smart bulb set to warm mode, or a Himalayan salt lamp all work and cost very little. No screens after 9 PM, or use blue-light blocking glasses.

Bedroom: 65 to 68 degrees Fahrenheit. Blackout curtains or a sleep mask. White noise if needed. Bed is for sleep and sex only.

Before bed: Write one priority for tomorrow. Research found that writing a specific to-do list before bed reduced time to fall asleep compared to journaling about completed tasks. Avoid alcohol within 3 hours of bed, as it suppresses cortisol initially, then produces a rebound spike in the second half of the night.

The 3 AM Wake

As progesterone declines, its brain metabolite allopregnanolone drops with it, removing the buffer against the natural cortisol rise that occurs around 2 to 3 AM. A subtle physiological signal becomes a full wake trigger. A small protein and fat snack such as nuts, nut butter, or full-fat Greek yogurt 30 to 60 minutes before bed can stabilize overnight glucose and reduce these wakings. If you wake and cannot return to sleep within 20 minutes, get up, go to a dim room, do something calm, and return when sleepy. This protects the association between your bed and sleep.

For women with persistent 3 AM waking, five evidence-based strategies are worth discussing with your clinician:

  1. Hormone support (Rx required) — For women with progesterone decline driving 3 AM waking, hormonal support options exist. Discuss with your healthcare provider.
  2. CBT-I — The most durable solution. Rebuilds sleep drive and breaks conditioned wakefulness through sleep restriction and stimulus control. Find a provider: behavioralsleep.org/find-a-provider
  3. Magnesium Glycinate — Supports GABA, moderates cortisol, and lowers core body temperature via glycine. Discuss dosing with your provider.
  4. Stabilize Blood Glucose Before Bed — A nocturnal glucose drop triggers cortisol. A small protein and fat snack 30 to 60 minutes before bed can blunt the surge.
  5. Anchor Your Circadian Rhythm — Same wake time daily. Morning bright light within 30 minutes of waking. No screens after 9 PM. Consider red bulbs or dimmers in lamps.
Sleep is a heavy topic, especially when you are already in the thick of a hard season. If you are struggling, you are not alone in that, and seeking help is not weakness. Because sleep is so multifactorial, it is often one of the last things to fully resolve. Be patient with yourself and honest with your provider.

09
Reduce or eliminate alcohol
Sleep architecture · Cortisol · Carcinogenesis · Hot flashes · Liver metabolism

Alcohol is the habit most likely to be quietly undermining everything else on this list. It disrupts sleep architecture, specifically suppressing REM and slow-wave sleep in the second half of the night. This is why a glass of wine helps you fall asleep and then wakes you at 2am. It raises cortisol. It worsens hot flashes and night sweats. It is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen by the World Health Organization.

The narrative that a glass of wine is good for your heart has been substantially revised in recent research. Current evidence does not support a safe threshold for women, and the risks increase in perimenopause when the liver’s estrogen metabolism is already under demand.

Return to the mindset shift: Own the choice. Instead of “I can’t drink wine,” try “I don’t want alcohol right now.” The reframe matters.

For many women in this season, this one is the hardest habit on the list. The process might take time and nothing has to be absolute. Decide what works for you and move toward it intentionally. If you feel you need support, please reach out to your primary care provider.
10
Digital boundaries
Cortisol · HPA axis · Attention restoration · Dopamine · Cognitive load

Screens are not neutral. Every notification, scroll, and news headline activates a low-grade stress response. Over the course of a day, constant digital noise keeps cortisol elevated, depletes directed attention, and crowds out the mental space needed for presence, creativity, and genuine rest.

Evening screen use specifically delays melatonin onset by up to two to three hours, meaning the scrolling that feels like unwinding is actively delaying the sleep your body needs.

Doom scrolling exploits the brain’s threat-detection system. The algorithm is designed to keep you engaged, surfacing content that triggers anxiety, outrage, or comparison. Each scroll is a small cortisol hit accumulated over an evening.

Digital boundaries are not self-denial. They are self-respect. Decide what deserves your attention and protect that decision.

Ask yourself once a day: is this adding to my life, or am I in default mode? That question alone is enough to begin.
11
Scheduled worry — all-day practice
Cognitive load · CBT · Implementation intention research · HRV · Sleep onset latency

The mental load of this season is not a personality flaw or a lack of discipline. It is a neurological reality. Holding unresolved tasks, worries, and decisions in working memory consumes significant mental resources, crowding out presence, creativity, and joy.

Scheduled worry time is a validated CBT technique. It works because it gives the anxious brain a later container. The worry does not disappear. It gets a meeting. Write it down, assign it a time, close the notebook, and return to now.

A physical notebook on the counter with a pencil already there removes all friction from capture. Research on implementation intentions shows that the more specific the system, the higher the follow-through. When a worry interrupts a good moment, write it and return. The notebook stays open. The present moment stays protected.

This. Is. Gold.

Fully present with my people
Social isolation · Longevity · Oxytocin · Inflammation · Mortality risk

Social isolation increases all-cause mortality risk comparably to smoking in longitudinal research. Perimenopause symptoms, fatigue, anxiety, irritability, low mood, can quietly drive withdrawal from the people and activities that sustain us. Connection is not a luxury. It is a health intervention.

When home with family, be home. Phone face-down, fully present, engaged. Real conversations with friends, not texts, not likes, and a monthly commitment that gets protected on the calendar.

Presence produces peace. And peace opens the door to laughter, to play, to the kind of joy that makes doing all of the above not just possible, but worth it.
13
Show up and give
Longevity · Dementia risk · Cardiovascular mortality · Purpose and health outcomes

Purpose is not an accomplishment. It is not a goal you set and work toward. It is quite simply the uniqueness that is you making its mark on the world around you. Philosophers, social scientists and Biblical scripture are in agreement that the people who find the most meaning out of life, who live long healthy years with joy and peace, are those who have found a way to serve that feels genuine to themselves and does good for others.

Many of us naturally find ourselves in roles that are in service to others. The question we must ask ourselves is do we really see the divine in what we are doing? Do we offer ourselves without expecting anything in return? Do we give from a heart that is grateful to have the capacity to serve?

In order for us to truly share ourselves in any capacity, to give a smile to a stranger, cook a warm meal for our family, or do anything at all, we must examine our heart, find strength through spiritual formation, and lay a solid foundation for our bodies, minds and spirits. It is on this ground our roots dig deeper, so that our branches extend further and can carry more with greater ease.

The giving is the meaning. Purpose is now, in your ugly and your beautiful. Not someday, not after the next accomplishment, not when everything is finally in order. You speak, you move, you breathe, you ripple — and the world receives only what you can offer. Take care of you so that you can be fully you.
14
Grateful moments: right now I am grateful for…
Positive psychology · HPA axis · Cortisol · Neuroplasticity · Savoring

The word itself seems almost burned by the wellness industry. So I invite you to immediately take this a step deeper. Gratitude is not spiritual bypassing. It is not toxic positivity. It is not complacency.

Gratitude is the mind and soul’s worldview that is an ongoing decision to override the emotion of the moment in order to find the gift. It is being thankful for the physician as you are bleeding, even though the wound hurts like hell. The analogy extends to nearly everything. From not having a wound at all, a paper cut or an amputation.

It means that you acknowledge the Giver before considering the circumstance. Gratitude is a choice to see how all that we have been given, the good and the bad, is for our formation. It is choosing to see not just what lies right in front of us, but what is beneath it and how that shapes who we are today and who we are becoming.

Scripture states in 1 Thessalonians 5:18: be thankful in all circumstances, not for all circumstances. That distinction matters. You are not being asked to pretend your pain is not real. You are being asked to hold your pain inside a larger story. Philippians 4:6-7 carries this further: “Don’t worry about anything; instead, pray about everything. Tell God what you need, and thank him for all he has done. Then you will experience God’s peace, which exceeds anything we can understand. His peace will guard your hearts and minds as you live in Christ Jesus.”

In the same way that we train our bodies to stand upright and strong, we can also train our minds toward a posture of gratitude. Gratitude interrupts the rumination loop by redirecting attention before the thought spiral gains momentum. It is grounding for the soul.

This is the work of the wisdom years: not just what we are doing with our bodies, but who we are becoming.

Spiritual anchor
Spiritual wellbeing · Cortisol · Inflammation · Meaning · Circadian biology

The American College of Lifestyle Medicine recognizes spiritual wellbeing as integral to whole-person health. Regular spiritual practice is associated with lower cortisol, reduced inflammatory markers, better sleep quality, improved pain perception, lower rates of depression, and longer lifespan across multiple longitudinal studies.

For women in their 40s and 50s, in a season when roles are evolving, bodies are changing, and identity is shifting, spiritual grounding provides something no supplement or protocol can: a sense of being known, held, and accompanied through the transition.

You do not need to carve out new time. Stack it onto your morning sunlight. Ten minutes outside, light on your face, a prayer or a moment of gratitude or simply the quiet awareness that you are held. Worship music in the car. A Bible app verse while coffee brews. Sixty seconds of honest prayer while the house is still quiet.

“And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him.” — Romans 8:28
Prayerful living is a way of life, not a moment in time. Invite God in, every moment, every day.
